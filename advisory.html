Practice 01

Operating Model Advisory

The problem

Your sales team is closing deals your operations team cannot deliver.

The gap between what you are promising clients and what you are actually building is growing. Your best clients are starting to notice. The moment founders feel this most acutely is usually one of two things: a client you did not expect to lose just left, or there is growing friction between your sales and delivery teams, each working hard but pulling in different directions.

This is not a sales problem. It is not an operations problem. It is a coherence problem, and it lives in the gap between what your business promises and what it actually delivers.

Who this is for

Companies that have moved past proof-of-concept and are now pushing hard on revenue growth. The product works, the model is proven, and growth is starting to outpace what operations can absorb. These are good problems to have. They mean you are growing. And they are problems that can be fixed.

What changes when we work together

Before

  • Revenue is at risk whenever delivery falls short of what was sold
  • Churn arrives as a surprise, too late to prevent
  • Clients leave when the experience does not match the promise
  • Sales and delivery pull in different directions, handovers break down
  • People are slotted into roles, not matched to skills or ambition
  • Leadership spends its time firefighting

After

  • Revenue at risk becomes protected: what you deliver matches what you sold
  • Churn becomes manageable and measurable, not a surprise
  • Clients stay longer and spend more because the experience matches the promise
  • Sales and delivery align: shared goals, clear ownership, handovers that work
  • Your people are in the right roles, skills matched to responsibilities
  • Leadership leads, not firefights

Practice 02

AI Readiness Advisory

The problem

Everyone feels behind on AI. Most are reacting to FOMO, not a goal.

Big companies can afford to throw budget at the AI movement and write off whatever does not work. Most SMBs cannot. The pressure to "do something with AI" keeps building anyway. That pressure usually shows up as a tool decision before a goal exists: a chatbot, a copilot, an automation, because a competitor mentioned it, or because standing still feels risky.

A few months later: a pilot nobody adopted, a subscription nobody uses, and no clearer sense of what it was supposed to fix. This is not a technology problem. It is a clarity problem, and it lives in the gap between adopting AI and knowing what it is actually for.

Who this is for

SMB owners and leadership teams who know AI matters but do not yet have a clear view of where it fits. You do not need a data science team or a six-figure budget. You need a plan grounded in your actual goals, built before any tool gets chosen. You do not have to be an AI expert. You just need someone who will ask the right questions before recommending anything.

What changes when we work together

Before

  • Tools get picked before anyone agrees on the goal
  • Leadership expects results before anyone has agreed what "done" looks like
  • "Success" is whatever feels impressive in a demo
  • The tool gets chosen first, the fit gets discovered later
  • Training is an afterthought, bolted on once the tool is already live
  • Adoption fades once the vendor moves on to the next deal

After

  • Goals come first: every tool decision traces back to a business outcome
  • Timeline and expectations are set early, so leadership knows what is coming
  • Success is defined and measured against operating costs, revenue, or productivity
  • The right tool is recommended deliberately, researched against your goals
  • A real adoption plan is built upfront with the people who will use it daily
  • Adoption is monitored and handed off properly to a team that knows how to keep it working
